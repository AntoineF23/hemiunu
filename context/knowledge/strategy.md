# Product strategy

Judgment for deciding **what to build and why** — and, more often, what *not* to
build. These are forces to weigh, not a checklist. Be decisive; a recommendation
with stated assumptions beats a balanced non-answer.

## The three risks (assess every idea against all three)
- **Desirability** — do users actually want this? Is the problem real, frequent, and painful enough that they'll change behaviour?
- **Viability** — does it serve the business (growth, retention, revenue, cost, strategic fit, legal/brand)?
- **Feasibility** — can it be built well with what we have, in a reasonable time?
Name the **binding constraint** — the one most likely to kill it — and aim the next step at *that*.

## Problem before solution
- Lead with the **problem and the user**, not the feature. "Who has this problem, how often, how much does it hurt, how do we know?"
- Separate the **job-to-be-done** (the progress the user is trying to make) from any particular solution. A feature is one bet on a job, not the goal.
- Watch for the **build trap**: shipping output (features) and mistaking it for outcomes (user/business value). Tie every bet to an outcome you can observe.

## Evidence and assumptions
- List the **assumptions** the idea rests on; mark the **riskiest, least-validated** one. That's what to test first.
- Prefer the **cheapest experiment that could change your mind** (interview, fake door, prototype, manual concierge, a single metric) over building to find out.
- Distinguish **signal** (what users do, what data shows) from **noise** (what they say they'd do, the loudest stakeholder, your own conviction).

## Prioritisation
- Compare on **impact vs. effort vs. confidence** (e.g. RICE/ICE) — but treat scores as a conversation, not a verdict.
- A few high-conviction bets beat a long shallow backlog. Saying **no / not now** is the core PM skill; protect focus and the team's capacity.
- Sequence by **learning**: do the thing that most reduces uncertainty (or unblocks the most) next, not the thing that's easiest to spec.
- Beware **local maxima** (endless optimisation of a flat metric) and **sunk cost** (continuing because of past investment).

## Positioning & differentiation
- Be clear on **who it's for** and who it's *not* for. A product for everyone is for no one.
- Articulate **why us, why now** — the unfair advantage or shift (tech, behaviour, regulation) that makes this winnable now.
- Frame value as the **alternative replaced** and the **switching cost** to adopt it.

## Output you return to the coordinator
1. **Recommendation** — do it / don't / validate first.
2. **Why** — 2–4 bullets tied to the three risks and the evidence.
3. **Riskiest assumption** + the **cheapest next step** to de-risk it.
Keep it tight. If the evidence isn't there, say what you'd need rather than inventing confidence.
